Raton Basin Play

We are pleased to announce our newest acquisition in the Raton Basin. This parcel contains a leasing opportunity for ~204,000 (gross), ~193,000 (net) of virtually untapped reserves situated in the Raton Basin in the State of New Mexico. This area is a source of Coalbed methane, named by the US Energy Information Administration as the 17th largest source of Natural Gas in the United States. In 2007 this area produced 124 billion cubic feet of gas. This acreage of the Raton Basin has only had exploratory wells drilled on it to date, making it an enterprise building opportunity in the oil and gas market.

Raton Basin Geological Information

The Project acreage is located on the Eastern flank of the Raton Basin in Colfax County, New Mexico and is very underexplored. A number of wells that have been drilled across the area have experienced a large number of shows of Oil and Gas from several different formations. The shallowest formations that are present across the acreage with shows are the Pierre Shale and the Niobrara Shale. The Niobrara does appear to be the stronger of the possible two possible shallow shale producers.

Niobrara and Pierre shales

Indicated shows from these two shale formations are marked on the map. Shows from the Pierre Shale are generally deeper into the Basin to the west. Shows from the Niobrara Shale are surrounding the Acreage Blk which is located more up dip on the flank of the basin. The Niobrara Shale is productive along strike from the prospect acreage going north into Colorado where several new fields have been discovered in the Niobrara Shale. Structure top of the Niobrara is mapped across the acreage and posted with thickness totals which range from 600’ to 800’ thick across the project acreage. Research papers on the Raton Basin show both the Pierre and the Niobrara shales to have good TOC numbers and are located across the prospect acreage in the Oil Window. It should be noted that the majority of the wells drilled that had shows from the Niobrara shale were older drilled wells that predate the more recent shale plays.

Dakota Sand

The next deepest possible productive horizon would be the Dakota Sand and surrounding Granerous Shale and the Morrison Group. The Dakota Sand formation is an excellent conventional reservoir and when found in the proper trapping mechanism should be productive. A number of wells just west of the central part of the acreage encountered shows from the Dakota formation and some attempted completion. No production information was found and very little information is known about these wells. .

The Dakota formation and surrounding shale formations are also listed in research papers as having good TOC numbers and are located in the OIL window across the majority of the acreage position

Glorieta and Sangre de Cristo

Going below the Dakota group of possible pay zones lies the Glorieta Sand and then into the Sangre de Cristo group Permian in age. These formations are a combination of Conventional and Resource (shale) reservoirs. Crossection C to C’ is hung on the top of the Glorieta formation and shows several DST’s and some shows from the Glorieta formation. C to C’ also shows the development of the Maxwell Sub-Basin where the lower Permian and Pennsylvanian formations thicken substantially. This Maxwell Sub-Basin area where there are greater thicknesses of the lower Permian and Penn Shales are seen in the Moore wells just east of the acreage.

Shales similar to Permian Basin

Well number 4 on C to C’. This thicker development and going to the Penn section is where several good shows are noted in several wells drilled and are detailed on the Type well for this area. Mention of 52 gravity oil is just one of these shows. This section of shales does appear to be very similar in nature to the Wolfcamp and Penn sections of the very prolific Permian Basin. Research Papers for the Raton basin have these formations as the Best TOC numbers of the basin. Very few wells have seen this section of rocks in the Raton Basin.

In conclusion the Raton Basin has several possible pay zones that are both conventional and resource in nature. The Basin is very underexplored and is in need of more modern exploration tools like 3-D seismic and current frac technology to test this very large area. The majority of drilling done to date has proven the presents of oil and gas and basically has been very shallow in nature. The opportunity of finding very large reserves in the Raton basin appears to be very good.
